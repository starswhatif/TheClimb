Types of Climbing

“You cannot stay on the summit forever; you have to come down again. So why bother in the first place? Just this: What is above knows what is below, but what is below does not know what is above. One climbs, one sees. One descends, one sees no longer, but one has seen. There is an art of conducting oneself in the lower regions by the memory of what one saw higher up. When one can no longer see, one can at least still know.” René Daumal

Bouldering

Bouldering is the simplest form of climbing, focussing on solving problems without a rope. It presents more complex challenges that require greater technical and problem-solving abilities. ‘Dynos', where a climber uses momentum to get to the next hold (requiring explosive power), are commonly used.

Sport

Sport climbing relies on permanent anchors fixed to the rock. The focus is on challenging sequences, strength and endurance, and at times, committing to terrifying falls. Bolts are used to scale blank rock faces that were otherwise inaccessible before, allowing climbers to climb harder and faster, pushing the limits of human capacity.

Trad

Trad is a form of free climbing. It is defined by a strong focus on exploration and a strict dedication to leaving nature unblemished, to preserve the natural environment of the cliff face. The leader places his own protection as he ascends, providing an unparallel sense of adventure, freedom and self-sufficiency.

Famous Climbers

Adam Ondra is considered the strongest climber in the world. In September 2017, he celebrated the first ascent of the “Silence” route in Flatanger, Norway; the hardest sport climb in the world, with a suggested rating of 9c.

Alex Honnold is the only person in history to free solo Freerider 5.12d, a 3,000 foot El Capitan wall in Yosemite National Park. This is one of the most impressive ascent in the history of climbing. Free soloing is high risk; at the end of the day, it's either death or perfection.
